## Why Images Take Up So Much Space

Modern cameras and smartphones capture images at incredibly high resolutions. A typical iPhone 15 photo is around 4000×3000 pixels at 12 megapixels. Each pixel stores color data, and when you multiply millions of pixels together, the file size adds up fast.

Here's what's happening under the hood:

**Raw image data** for a 12MP photo is enormous — roughly 36MB if stored completely uncompressed. The JPEG format compresses this down to 3-8MB by removing fine details the human eye typically doesn't notice. But even that compressed JPEG contains more data than most use cases actually need.

If you're storing a photo to share on WhatsApp, post on Instagram, send by email, or keep as a digital memory — you don't need the full 6MB version. A well-compressed 800KB version looks identical on screen.

**The math:**
- 1,000 photos at 5MB each = **5GB of storage**
- 1,000 photos at 500KB each = **500MB of storage**
- **Savings: 4.5GB — with no visible quality loss**

That's the difference between a full storage notification and months of free space.

## Types of Image Compression

Understanding the two types helps you choose the right approach:

### Lossy Compression

Lossy compression permanently removes some image data to reduce file size. JPEG uses lossy compression — it analyzes the image and discards details that are statistically unlikely to be noticed by the human eye, like subtle color variations in areas of uniform color.

The key insight: at quality levels of 75-85%, lossy compression produces files that are indistinguishable from the originals to the human eye on a screen, at a fraction of the file size.

**Best for:** Photographs, social media images, web images, anything viewed on screens

### Lossless Compression

Lossless compression reduces file size without removing any data. The original image can be perfectly reconstructed from the compressed version. PNG uses lossless compression.

Lossless compression saves less space than lossy (typically 20-50% reduction vs 80-95% for lossy) but preserves every pixel exactly.

**Best for:** Logos, graphics with text, images requiring transparency, source files you'll edit later

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Compressing the same image twice.** Every time you re-compress a JPEG, quality degrades slightly. Always compress from the original. Keep originals in a separate folder if you need them later.

**Compressing images you'll need to edit.** If you plan to edit a photo in Photoshop or Lightroom, keep the original for editing. Compress only the final export.

**Using PNG for photos.** PNG is lossless and much larger than JPEG for photographs. If you're storing photos as PNG, converting them to JPEG alone saves 60-80% of space.

**Over-compressing text-heavy images.** Images containing text (screenshots of documents, infographics with small text) degrade faster than photos when compressed heavily. Keep these at 80%+ quality.
